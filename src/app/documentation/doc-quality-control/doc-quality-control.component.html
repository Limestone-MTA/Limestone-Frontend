Quality Control Step

Before diving into data analysis, it is essential to perform a comprehensive quality control process to ensure the reliability and accuracy of the results. Poor data quality can lead to biased insights, inaccurate conclusions, and flawed models.

Why Do You Need Quality Control?

Key Reasons for Performing Quality Control:

  • Ensure Data Integrity: Identify and remove erroneous or incomplete data that could compromise the analysis.
  • Improve Model Performance: Clean and consistent data lead to better-performing models and more reliable results.
  • Reduce Bias: Prevent incorrect inferences by addressing missing values, outliers, or inconsistent data points.
  • Enhance Interpretability: With clean data, it becomes easier to interpret relationships between variables and produce actionable insights.

Filtering Based on Missing Values

Missing values can introduce bias or reduce the power of your analysis. Ignoring missing data could lead to incorrect results or inaccurate models. Depending on the proportion and pattern of missingness, different strategies can be applied.
In Limestone you can set up a threshold to remove variables with more than a certain percentage of missing values.

Filtering Based on Outliers

Outliers are data points that deviate significantly from the majority of the dataset. They can distort statistical analyses, influence model coefficients, or lead to overfitting in machine learning models.
In Limestone, you can remove outliers who are more than a certain distance from the mean of the distribution, this distance is based on the standard deviation of the variable. The removed values will be changed to missing values.

Merging Categories

When analyzing categorical data, small sample sizes in certain categories can lead to unreliable or unstable results, especially in statistical models or machine learning algorithms. To address this, you can merge categories with very few samples into a broader or "other" category. This ensures that each category has a sufficient number of observations for meaningful analysis.
